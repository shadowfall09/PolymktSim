\section{Analysis}
\label{sec:analysis}

\subsection{Why Information Asymmetry Beats Homogeneous Debate}
\label{sec:why}

\input{figures/TABLE_ablation}

\paragraph{\ding{224} Creating asymmetry is the largest single factor.}
Table~\ref{tab:ablation} removes one designed component at a time.
The biggest degradation comes from removing information asymmetry --- giving every agent the full pool with full sharing, i.e., exactly the Standard Debate configuration with our prompt and aggregation: Brier worsens from 0.1647 to 0.1815 on \textsc{PolyGym} (paired $\Delta = -0.0169$, CI $[-0.0314, -0.0029]$) and from 0.2212 to 0.2433 on FutureX ($\Delta = -0.0221$, CI $[-0.0515, +0.0081]$), significant on \textsc{PolyGym} and directionally consistent on FutureX.
Because this comparison holds the prompt, protocol, and aggregation fixed, the gap over the strongest baseline is attributable to information structure alone.
Removing deliberation (round-1 forecasts only) costs 0.005 on \textsc{PolyGym} and 0.014 on FutureX ($\Delta = -0.0141$, CI $[-0.0325, +0.0027]$), and removing the shrinkage layer costs 0.007 / 0.021: partitioning creates the \emph{potential} for improvement, deliberation and calibrated aggregation \emph{realize} it.

\input{figures/TABLE_design_grid}

\paragraph{\ding{224} The advantage rests on the principle, not on a tuned hyperparameter.}
Table~\ref{tab:design} varies every remaining design dimension one at a time.
The default configuration is highly stable (Brier std $0.0002$ after shrinkage across 3 seeds), and against this yardstick every variation --- $\rho \in \{0.3, 0.7\}$, a third deliberation round, numeric-only sharing, random routing --- shifts Brier by at most $0.004$, within seed noise (all paired CIs vs.\ the default straddle zero).
The single catastrophic choice is $\rho{=}1$: full homogenization degrades Brier by $0.017$ and AUC from 0.757 to 0.711, the only significant change in the grid.
The component directions still agree with the theory --- a third round adds nothing once private signals have propagated ($R{=}3 \approx R{=}2$), and replacing rationale sharing or relevance routing lowers AUC (0.751 / 0.746 vs.\ 0.757) --- but their magnitudes are secondary.
What separates InfoDelphi from the baselines is therefore not a fragile hyperparameter setting but the presence of private evidence at all: any evidence partition beats no partition, and no setting of the other knobs rescues the homogeneous configuration.

\paragraph{\ding{224} Homogeneous deliberation herds; partitioned deliberation preserves diversity.}
Figure~\ref{fig:herding} traces \emph{why} the strongest multi-agent baseline loses to InfoDelphi despite an identical protocol.
In round 1 the two information structures show comparable per-question disagreement.
After one round of deliberation, however, disagreement under homogeneous input collapses by 74--79\% (e.g., 0.059 $\to$ 0.015 on \textsc{PolyGym}) and mean pairwise error correlation rises to 0.99: the panel has effectively merged into a single agent, and averaging its members can no longer cancel errors.
With partitioned evidence, agents converge far less (0.050 $\to$ 0.028) and retain lower error correlation (0.978), preserving the diversity term of Eq.~\eqref{eq:bvd}.
This is direct empirical evidence for the mechanism of Proposition~\ref{prop:decorrelation}: private evidence gives each agent an independent basis for conviction that social pressure alone cannot override.

\paragraph{\ding{224} Asymmetry makes agents confident about different things.}
A natural worry is that withholding evidence merely makes agents timid.
The opposite holds: after the final round, InfoDelphi's agents are as extreme as Standard Debate's (mean $|p - 0.5|$ of 0.277 vs.\ 0.271 on \textsc{PolyGym}), but their inter-agent variance is $2.7\times$ higher ($1.3\times$ on FutureX).
Information asymmetry does not reduce individual confidence --- it decorrelates its \emph{direction}, so the mean-pooling step of Section~\ref{sec:aggregation} averages genuinely complementary judgments rather than three copies of one belief.
This validates the evidence-routing design directly: the value of the partition lies in the diversity it protects.

\subsection{Calibration: A Shared Failure Mode, Corrected in Aggregation}
\label{sec:calibration_study}

\paragraph{\ding{224} The miscalibration is one-sided, motivating an asymmetric correction.}
Section~\ref{sec:results} showed every baseline overshooting on high-confidence YES forecasts.
Our raw ensemble inherits the same model-level bias~\citep{tian2023just}, but only on one side: forecasts below the prior ($p < 0.3$) and in the mid-band are close to calibrated (e.g., stated 0.42 vs.\ empirical 0.45 on \textsc{PolyGym}), while the affirmative band overshoots badly (stated 0.79 vs.\ hit rate 0.29 on \textsc{PolyGym}; 0.81 vs.\ 0.59 on FutureX).
This one-sidedness is precisely why Eq.~\eqref{eq:shrink} shrinks asymmetrically ($w_{\mathrm{hi}} < w_{\mathrm{lo}}$): the correction acts strongly where the errors live and leaves the calibrated region nearly untouched.
Symmetric alternatives --- shrinking everything toward 0.5, or prompt-level skepticism instructions --- depress the whole forecast distribution and can underperform a constant base-rate predictor (Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_promptcal}).

\paragraph{\ding{224} The correction transfers because the bias is model-level, not dataset-level.}
The three shrinkage parameters are fit on Polymarket and applied to FutureX unchanged, improving Brier from 0.2212 to 0.2006 and accuracy from 0.688 to 0.701; re-fitting on FutureX recovers the same setting.
A correction that transfers across question distributions with different base rates (0.232 vs.\ 0.355) is capturing a property of the forecaster, not an artifact of one dataset.
We also verify the comparison is not an artifact of the calibration layer itself: applying the identical transform to every baseline post hoc (Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_control}) helps them too, but InfoDelphi remains the best system on both datasets --- the residual advantage is exactly the part that calibration cannot supply, namely the decorrelated information structure of Section~\ref{sec:why}.
A companion study of the Sports domain (Appendix~\ref{sec:sports}) probes the boundary of this advantage: information asymmetry improves the \emph{utilization} of informative evidence, but cannot create information the corpus does not contain.
